# GTCX as User-Owned Infrastructure: The DTCC Model

## Executive Summary

**The question:** Could TradePass and GeoTag operate as user-owned utilities rather than venture-backed companies?

**The answer:** Yes — and this model may be _more_ aligned with the mission, _more_ attractive to governments, and _more_ sustainable long-term than traditional equity financing.

**The insight:** DTCC processes $2.4 quadrillion annually because it's owned by the institutions that depend on it. They trust it _because_ no single entity controls it. The same logic applies to identity and location verification infrastructure for commodity markets.

---

## Part 1: Understanding the DTCC Model

### What DTCC Actually Is

The Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation is:

- **User-owned**: 50+ banks and broker-dealers own it
- **Utility-priced**: Fees cover costs + modest reserves, not profit maximization
- **Systemically critical**: Processes virtually all US securities transactions
- **Neutrally governed**: No single owner dominates
- **Regulated but independent**: SEC oversight, but operationally autonomous

### Why It Works

| Factor                    | How DTCC Benefits                              | How GTCX Would Benefit                                           |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Trust**                 | No competitor controls your data               | Governments trust infrastructure they partly own                 |
| **Alignment**             | Users want low costs and reliability           | Producers, buyers, and regulators all want accurate verification |
| **Stability**             | No exit pressure, no acquisition risk          | Infrastructure persists regardless of market cycles              |
| **Network effects**       | More users = more valuable                     | Universal identity/location = maximum interoperability           |
| **Regulatory acceptance** | Easier to regulate a utility than a monopolist | Governments more likely to mandate use of shared infrastructure  |

### What DTCC Is NOT

- Not a non-profit (it generates surplus)
- Not a government agency (privately owned)
- Not democratically governed (ownership proportional to usage)
- Not slow to innovate (significant R&D budget)
- Not charity (users pay market-rate fees)

## Part 4: Economics Comparison

### Traditional VC Model

```
Revenue: Transaction fees + SaaS
Goal: Maximize revenue for equity exit
Fees: What market will bear ($0.25-1.00/verification)
Exit: IPO or acquisition at 10-20x revenue
Founder outcome: Equity liquidity event
User outcome: Vendor dependency
```

**18-Month Raise:** $3.5M (TradePass) or $3.2M (GeoTag)
**Expected Return:** 10-20x for investors
**Exit Timeline:** 5-7 years

### User-Owned Utility Model

```
Revenue: Transaction fees (cost-plus)
Goal: Reliable service at lowest sustainable cost
Fees: Cost + 10-15% reserve ($0.05-0.15/verification)
Exit: None — perpetual infrastructure
Founder outcome: Service contracts + Class B shares
User outcome: Ownership + lower costs
```

**Initial Funding:** $4-6M (founding member contributions + DFI grants)
**Expected Return:** Cost savings to members (not investor returns)
**Exit Timeline:** Never — infrastructure persists

### Fee Comparison

| Model                  | Fee per Verification | Annual Cost (100K verifications) |
| ---------------------- | -------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| VC-backed (aggressive) | $0.50                | $50,000                          |
| VC-backed (moderate)   | $0.25                | $25,000                          |
| **User-owned utility** | $0.08                | $8,000                           |
| Cost floor             | $0.05                | $5,000                           |

**Key insight:** User-owned model can charge 3-5x less because there's no equity return requirement.

### DTCC-Style Board Structure

```
GTCX Utility Board (11 seats)

Producer Representatives (3 seats)
├── West Africa Cooperatives (elected)
├── East Africa Cooperatives (elected)
└── Other Regions (rotating)

Buyer Representatives (2 seats)
├── Refineries (elected by volume)
└── Trading Houses (elected by volume)

Government Representatives (2 seats)
├── African Union nominee
└── Rotating national governments

DFI Representatives (2 seats)
├── Lead DFI investor
└── Rotating DFI seat

Independent Directors (2 seats)
├── Technical/Security expert
└── Governance/Policy expert
```

### Decision Rights

| Decision Type                       | Required Majority           |
| ----------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Operational (fees, services)        | Simple majority             |
| Strategic (new products, regions)   | 2/3 majority                |
| Constitutional (governance changes) | 3/4 majority + ratification |
| Dissolution                         | 90% + regulatory approval   |

### Checks on Power

1. **No single stakeholder class can dominate** — producers, buyers, and governments each have veto power on constitutional matters

2. **Regional balance required** — no single country can have >25% of governance weight

3. **Founder lockup** — founding team Class B shares have no governance rights for 7 years

4. **Regulatory oversight** — Swiss foundation law + African Union framework

## Part 11: Risks and Mitigations

### Risk: "This is too slow for the market opportunity"

**Mitigation:** The opposite may be true. Government partnerships close faster when you offer ownership rather than dependency. Corporate founding members commit faster when they see governance rights.

### Risk: "DFIs won't fund this structure"

**Mitigation:** This is _exactly_ what DFIs fund in other sectors (agricultural cooperatives, microfinance networks, rural utilities). Frame it as cooperative infrastructure, not "company that doesn't want VC."

### Risk: "Founders won't be motivated without equity upside"

**Mitigation:** The economics show comparable or better lifetime earnings. And for mission-driven founders, the permanence of user-owned infrastructure IS the motivation.

### Risk: "Governance will be too slow"

**Mitigation:**

- Operational decisions by management (simple majority board approval)
- Only constitutional changes require supermajority
- Professional management team (not volunteer committee)
- Clear decision rights matrix

### Risk: "What if a big player tries to take over?"

**Mitigation:**

- No single member can hold >15% ownership
- Constitutional changes require 3/4 supermajority
- Swiss foundation has anti-takeover provisions in charter
- IP is held by foundation, not operating company

## Part 12: The Decision Framework

### When to Choose VC-Backed

Choose traditional equity if:

- Speed to market is critical (18 months matters)
- Founder exit is a priority
- Willing to accept acquisition/IPO as outcome
- Believe you can build defensible moat before competition

### When to Choose User-Owned

Choose utility model if:

- Permanence matters more than speed
- Mission alignment is non-negotiable
- Government trust is critical for adoption
- Willing to accept slower personal wealth accumulation
- Believe network effects require neutral ownership

### The GTCX-Specific Answer

Given:

- "Building cathedrals, not companies"
- Ubuntu Economics as core philosophy
- Government partnerships as primary distribution
- Infrastructure that should outlive founders
- Mission to empower producers, not extract from them

**The user-owned utility model is more aligned with everything GTCX claims to stand for.**

The question isn't "can we do this?" — it's "why would we do anything else?"
