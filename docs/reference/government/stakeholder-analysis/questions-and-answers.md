# Questions and Answers

### Critical Stakeholder Questions and Answers

- “How do you verify the gold’s source?” Governments and buyers will demand chain-of-custody proof. AGX must use unique IDs or tags on each batch of gold (e.g. sealed envelopes, laser-engraved bar codes, or chemical “fingerprinting” of gold ore). Each batch should be logged on the blockchain with miner identity and certificate. Like Ghana’s plan, AGX could require every batch to have a “unique digital code” linked to a miner’s license. Field audits and random inspections (as Fenix’s experience showed) are critical to deter false claims. Partnering with certification programs (e.g. Fairmined) can help: Fairmined’s third-party audits and premiums reinforce correct sourcing, although AGX must be wary of weak “certifications” that lack rigorous checks.
- “Is tokenizing gold legal?” Regulators will ask whether tokens are recognized and how they are regulated. The answer varies by country, but precedents exist: in the UAE, DMCC’s Comtech Gold Tokens (CGO) are legally backed by real gold bars with trade warrants, and Dubai’s regulators have embraced tokenized assets. In South Africa, assets like Krugerrands are legal tender, and Mesh.trade operated under local financial authority registrations. AGX must clarify with local legal counsel whether tokens are deemed securities, commodities, or something else, and ensure compliance (e.g. register as exchanges or custodians where needed). If uncertainties remain, AGX should aim for regulatory sandboxes or phased rollouts (e.g. start as a gold custodian before issuing tokens).
- “What prevents fraud or double-spending?” Stakeholders worry that a miner or middleman might sell the same gold twice via tokens. AGX’s compliance protocol must enforce proof-of-custody: gold enters only through AGX-approved vaults, which mint tokens only when physical gold is received and verified. Strong audit trails are needed: Vaults should use high-assurance hardware and allow surprise inspections by independent third-parties. The blockchain can timestamp every transaction, preventing double use of codes. Similar to Paxos enabling holders to lookup the serial of their gold bar, AGX could allow any token holder to verify the specific bar and vault backing their tokens.
- “How is price determined and can we trust it?” If an exchange is launched, miners and buyers will ask how token prices relate to spot. AGX must tie token prices to transparent benchmarks (e.g. LBMA gold price), adjusting for local premiums or quality. For example, Mesh.trade updated token price daily to match Krugerrand spot. AGX should publish oracles for gold price feeds and platform fees. Disputes over pricing can be minimized if smart contracts automatically adjust to market data.<br>
- “Who governs the system?” Trust issues loom: if AGX is privately run, governments may fear loss of control; if government-run, investors may fear politicization. Clarity is needed: AGX should have a transparent governance structure. Perhaps a multi-stakeholder consortium (including government representatives, mining associations, and independent auditors) could oversee the protocol rules and vet node operators. The African Diamond Council’s Authentia initiative shows how industry bodies can lead traceability efforts. AGX’s governance could similarly involve a council of African mining regulators or an NGO like Swissaid (author of the smuggling report) to enforce standards. Data input (who records transactions) also needs oversight: mining associations or PMMC-like entities should validate initial entries.<br>
- “What if technology fails or is hacked?” Stakeholders know blockchain networks can have outages or smart contract bugs. AGX must assume “tech failure” scenarios: keep offline backups of transaction logs, insurance against hacking losses, and manual override processes. Custody risks (physical theft) compound cybersecurity risks. Lessons from crypto lapses (e.g. hack of a large exchange) suggest multi-signature vault access, cold-storage for keys, and routine security audits.<br>
- “Is it legal for us to buy/sell these tokens?” Regulatory compliance extends to end-users. Institutional buyers (banks, funds) will ask if they can hold gold-tokens and if it counts as “reserved” gold. In many jurisdictions, token-held gold would still be considered gold for reserve calculations if properly structured (see Paxos, whose PAXG tokens represent allocated gold). AGX should clarify with central banks and tax authorities how token trades are treated for foreign exchange rules, and ensure KYC/AML checks on token traders. The platform could restrict transfers to KYC’d entities (like how Paxos limits PAXG creation/redemption to approved accounts) to satisfy regulators.<br>
- “How do you handle corrupt officials?” Governments and investors know corruption is rampant in mining and customs. If AGX doesn’t address this, tokens could just become an alternate money-laundering route. Robust KYC for miners and buyers is vital. Auditing by external agencies (e.g. TRACE, Deloitte, etc.) and publishing compliance reports can reduce risk. Digital receipts (as in Brazil’s new law) are better than paper, but unless AGX’s blockchain records are public, suspicion remains. Opening certain data to the public (anonymized) or allowing NGOs to spot-check can increase credibility.
