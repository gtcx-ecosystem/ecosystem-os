# Governance, Trust, and Data Integrity

- Who Runs AGX?: A crucial trust issue is governance. If a single company or foreign entity runs it, African stakeholders will be uneasy. Ideally, a neutral international body or African consortium should govern AGX. For example, DMCC (a government agency) partnered to create UAE tokens, giving governments confidence. AGX could be co-owned by an African Union trust or a consortium of mining ministries.
- Auditability: Blockchain can log everything, but data must be verified at entry. AGX must set up an official process for vetting miners and transactions. Perhaps national “Gold Boards” or “Precious Metals Authorities” (like Ghana’s PMMC) could validate new miner registrations and initial production data, then feed the verified data into AGX’s protocol. This prevents fake data flooding the chain.
- Data Privacy vs Transparency: Buyers want proof of origin; miners want privacy for commercial data. AGX might use permissioned chains or zero-knowledge proofs: essential transaction data (weights, dates, certifications) can be public or viewable by regulators, while private details (miner identity) are shielded. This must be handled delicately to maintain trust without exposing traders to risks (e.g. theft based on disclosed holdings).
- Enforcement: Who punishes bad actors on AGX? If a miner falsifies data, what’s the penalty? AGX rules must be backed by legal enforcement – possibly contracts that allow seizure of assets, or blacklisting of users in the consortium network. Partnering with law enforcement (e.g. joint task forces as Ghana plans) can give teeth.
