# Business Sustainability & Monetization

- Revenue Model: Likely fees on transactions, custody, and token issuance/redemption. AGX must balance fees so they cover costs (vaults, audits, tech) but don’t drive users away. For instance, Mesh.trade likely charges spreads or custody fees. AGX should transparently itemize its fees (e.g. 0.x% per trade, plus 0.x% custody), benchmarked to global standards. Long-term sustainability may also come from value-added services (e.g. data subscription, lending programs for tokens).<br>
- Adoption and Liquidity: An exchange needs volume. Early on, AGX may subsidize market-making or partner with big buyers (e.g. large gold purchasers in India or China) to ensure liquidity. Without volume, prices will be volatile and spread too wide, deterring usage. Initial listing of high-profile gold products (e.g. local mint gold coins, or refined bars with known quality) can anchor the market.<br>
- Competition: AGX faces competition from traditional bullion markets and any future African exchanges. If global tokenization of gold expands (as seen in Dubai’s push), AGX must differentiate (e.g. by focusing on African-origin gold and local empowerment). Leveraging African diaspora and gold ETFs might help (minting tokens that wrap gold plus African development bonds, for example).<br>
- Scale and Growth: Sustainability means eventually scaling beyond core markets. AGX should plan for integration with global commodity networks. For example, if Shanghai or London exchanges show interest (China is promoting SGE internationalization), AGX tokens might even trade abroad, increasing arbitrage liquidity but complicating regulation.
