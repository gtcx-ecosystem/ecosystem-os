**8:30 AM - 12:00 PM** — Physical verification. This is the hard work. If it's bagged goods — coffee, rice — he's counting bags, checking stack configurations, pulling random samples. If it's bulk — maize in a silo — he's taking depth measurements, doing calculations. He's also checking for tampering, water damage, pest infestation. Taking photos of everything.

**12:00 PM** — Quick lunch, usually on-site. Maybe the warehouse provides something, maybe he brought his own.

**1:00 PM - 3:00 PM** — Documentation. He's reviewing warehouse receipts, checking them against physical stock. Verifying the trader's records match what's actually there. If there's a discrepancy, he's investigating — did stock move? Was there spoilage? Is someone lying?

**3:00 PM** — Wraps up, gets signatures from the warehouse manager confirming his visit and findings.

**4:00 PM** — Drives back to Dar. Probably takes calls on the road — coordinating tomorrow's schedule.

**6:00 PM** — Back at home or a local office. Writes up his report. Uploads photos. Sends to our operations team who format it for the client.

**Next day** — The bank gets a PDF report with photos, quantities, quality assessment, and any concerns flagged.

Some days he might do two smaller sites. Some days it's one big facility that takes the whole day. Vessel inspections at ports are different again — those can run overnight if a ship is loading.

## 4. What tools do your inspectors use today?

_[slight laugh]_

Honestly? It's a mix. We've modernized some things, but we're not a tech company.

**Standard kit:**

- **Smartphone** — for photos, GPS coordinates, sometimes voice notes
- **Notebook** — yes, physical notebook. Old habits die hard, and it works when the phone dies
- **Measuring equipment** — tape measures, sometimes laser distance meters for bigger facilities
- **Sampling tools** — probes for grain, moisture meters, sometimes we send samples to labs
- **Calculator** — volume-to-tonnage conversions
- **Printed forms** — inspection checklists, sign-off sheets

**Reporting:**
Most guys type up their reports in Word or fill in Excel templates. Upload photos separately. Our ops team consolidates into a client-ready PDF.

**What we don't have:**
We don't have a proper mobile app. We've talked about it. Dheerie wants it. But building software isn't our core competence, and the off-the-shelf stuff doesn't quite fit our workflows. The quotes we've gotten are expensive, and our margins don't leave a lot of room for big tech investments.

**The gap:**
Look, I'll be honest — the biggest vulnerability is the time lag between inspection and report. If an inspector does a site visit on Tuesday, the bank might not see the report until Thursday or Friday. In that window, things could change. We mitigate it with CMAs where we control the site, but for standalone inspections, there's a trust gap.

The other issue is photo integrity. We've had situations — not at SIS, but I've heard industry stories — where photos get reused or manipulated. We trust our people, but the system relies on that trust. There's no technical proof that a photo was taken at a specific place and time.
