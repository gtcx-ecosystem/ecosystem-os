# ESG & Conflict Risk

Artisanal gold mining in Ghana often involves rudimentary methods (e.g. panning or sluicing with mercury) that pose serious health and environmental risks. Minimizing these hazards is a priority – Ghana has partnered with UNDP to promote mercury-free mining in line with the Minamata Convention. Under AGX protocol, any gold linked to mercury use, child labor, or protected-area mining is automatically excluded. All supplies must pass ESG due diligence before entering the chain.

The protocol institutes mandatory Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) screening at the source: every mine or mine-sector supplier is evaluated for legal compliance and risk factors. Key screening criteria include:

- Environmental Risks: No mining or trading of gold that involved illegal activities (e.g. unauthorized deforestation, mining in protected forests or waterway buffers). Explicitly prohibited is open use of mercury or other toxic chemicals beyond what is allowed under Ghana’s environmental laws. (Ghana is a party to the Minamata Convention and is actively promoting mercury-free ASM.) Gold from operations causing “irremediable environmental degradation” is disqualified.
- Social Risks: No use of child labor or forced labor. Ghanaian law (Children’s Act 1998) and international ILO standards forbid children under 18 in mining. In practice, thousands of children still work in Ghana’s gold mines despite these laws. Any evidence of underage workers at a mine (even familial help) is an immediate red flag. Likewise, evidence of unsafe or exploitative labor (lack of PPE, excessive hours, trafficked workers) must exclude that source. The protocol also checks for community grievance: mining operations must have documentation of land rights and local consent (meeting Ghanaian land tenure laws and free, prior, informed consent for communities).
- Conflict Risks: While Ghana is generally stable, the protocol extends due diligence to global conflict zones. Suppliers or shipments routed through Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas (CAHRAs) require extra scrutiny per OECD guidance. For example, gold from neighboring countries where rebel activity is known (e.g. DRC, South Sudan) must be scrutinized: the protocol calls for the OECD 5-step due diligence process (mapping, risk ID, mitigation, etc.) for any such material. In addition, AGX will blacklist gold that matches known conflict smuggling patterns. (Recent reports note that conflict gold from Central/East Africa is often funneled through Dubai. Weak enforcement in some trade centers has enabled illicit flows.) The protocol therefore requires certification of origin and chain-of-custody to prove gold is conflict-free.

Red flags triggering rejection include any indication of mercury use, operation in a protected or conflict zone, missing environmental permits, evidence of child workers, or unexplained shipments. Suppliers must demonstrate compliance with the OECD Due Diligence Guidance and the UN Guiding Principles on Business & Human Rights (both referenced in Ghana’s Bank of Ghana policy). All gold must be responsibly sourced; AGX will refuse any consignment failing these screens. In practice, this means miners/exporters must furnish environmental compliance certificates and social audits along with mineral paperwork.
